Mixed Models in R

Introduction

Overview

Mixed models are an extremely useful modeling tool for correlated data, where the correlation typically arises from the observations being clustered in some way. It is quite common to have data in which we have repeated measurements for the units of observation, or in which the units of observation are otherwise clustered (e.g.Â students within school, cities within geographic region). While there are different ways to approach such a situation, mixed models are a very common and powerful tool to do so.

Goals

The goal of this workshop is primarily to provide a sense of when one would use mixed models and a variety of standard techniques. Additionally, weâll have exercises to practice.

Prerequisites

The document is for the most part very applied in nature, and only assumes a basic understanding of standard regression models. Use of R for basic regression modeling is also assumed. Demonstrations will be done almost entirely with the lme4 package.

Terminology

For the uninitiated, the terminology surrounding mixed models, especially across disciplines, can be a bit daunting.

Some terms you might come across regarding these types of models:

  • Variance components
  • Random intercepts and slopes
  • Random effects
  • Random coefficients
  • Varying coefficients
  • Intercepts and slopes-as-outcomes
  • Hierarchical linear models
  • Multilevel models (implies multiple levels of hierarchically clustered data)
  • Growth curve models (possibly Latent GCM)
  • Mixed effects models

All describe types of mixed models. Some might be more historical, others are more often seen in a specific discipline, others might refer to a certain data structure (e.g.Â multilevel clustering), and still others are special cases. Mixed effects, or simply mixed, models generally refer to a mixture of fixed and random effects. I prefer the term mixed models because it is simple and no specific structure is implied. Fixed effects, is perhaps a poor but nonetheless stubborn term for the typical main effects one would see in a linear regression model, i.e.Â the non-random part of a mixed model, and in some contexts they are referred to as the population average effect.

Kinds of clustering

Data might have one or multiple sources of clustering, and that clustering may be hierarchical, such that clusters are nested within other clusters. An example would be scholastic aptitude tests given multiple times to students (repeated observations nested within students, students nested within schools, schools nested within districts). In other cases, there is no nesting structure. An example would be a reaction time experiment where participants perform the same set of tasks. While observations are nested within individual, observations are also clustered according to task type. Some use the terms nested and crossed to distinguish between these scenarios. In addition clustering may be balanced or not. We might expect more balance in studies of an experimental nature, but definitely not in other cases, e.g.Â where the cluster is something like geographical unit and the observations are people.

However, itâs important to get a sense of the flexibility of mixed models to handle a variety of data situations.
